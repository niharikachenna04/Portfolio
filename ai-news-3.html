The Future Risk of AGI and AI Superintelligence

Forbes article by Lance B. Eliot | October 12, 2025

View Source
Summary

Summary of the Article

The article explains that Artificial General Intelligence (AGI) and Artificial Superintelligence (ASI) may not automatically unite society. Instead, these advanced AI systems could create divisions because different groups may trust different AI outputs and systems.

A major idea is that people may begin treating AI as an “ultimate authority.” If one group trusts one AI system and another group trusts a different one, conflicts may arise over which AI is correct, who controls it, and how much authority it should have.

The article suggests that AI could amplify social and political divisions instead of solving them. The real challenge is not just the technology itself, but how humans respond to it, including issues of trust, governance, and ethical responsibility.

Key Insight

The real challenge is not only advanced AI, but how humans choose to trust and use it.

Without proper control and transparency, AI could increase conflict instead of improving collaboration.

Important Takeaways

Key Ideas from the Article

AI as Authority

People may rely on AI systems for major decisions in policy, business, and daily life.

Competing AI Systems

Different groups may follow different AI systems, leading to disagreement.

Social Fragmentation

AI could increase divisions instead of creating consensus.

Power and Control

Organizations controlling AI may gain major influence.

Impact

Impact on Society

The article highlights that AGI and ASI could create political and economic challenges. Governments may compete for AI dominance, and automation may increase inequality.

Another concern is the shift in authority. People may depend on AI for legal, medical, and business decisions, raising questions about accountability and trust.

The key takeaway is that the future of AI depends on human governance and responsible use.

AEC Relevance

Why This Matters to Construction

In construction, AI can support scheduling, cost estimation, design optimization, and safety prediction. However, relying too much on AI raises concerns about responsibility.

Different firms using different AI tools could lead to inconsistent standards and workflows. This may create challenges in collaboration and project coordination.

Professionals may shift from decision-makers to AI supervisors who validate outputs and ensure ethical use.

Reflection

AI should support professionals, not replace decision-making.
